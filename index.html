Hero · Sec 2a

Narrative / Short Story

Pick when the prompt gives you a situation, an image, or the first/last line.

Conventions
  • Clear arc — hook, rising action, turn, resolution
  • Single controlling point of view, held throughout
  • Sensory imagery over plot summary; show, don't tell
  • Dialogue that carries character, not exposition
  • Time markers and tense discipline (usually past)
  • Symbolic object or motif that closes the loop
  • Opening hook and resonant, earned final line
The Last Tiffin a narrative · 358 words

The platform at Dadar smelled of diesel and wet newsprint, and Lata's steel dabba clicked against her wrist like a small, impatient heart. 1Her mother's hands had folded the roti that morning the way they folded everything — carefully, as if grief were a crease that could be ironed out. She was seventeen, and today was the first day she would deliver tiffins alone.

The 7:48 local hissed in. She climbed on with the other dabbawalas, the carriage swallowing her whole in a warm blur of sweat, cardamom and yesterday's rain. A man in a frayed collar made room without looking up. In Bombay, kindness was a thing people did with their elbows.

The tiffins had to reach Nariman Point by noon. Her father had run this route for twenty-two years. He had left it to her the way other fathers left jewellery — heavier than it looked, and impossible to resize. She traced the code on each lid with her thumb: the red circle for a lawyer, the blue dash for a bank clerk, the black cross for the old widow in building nine who only ate what her dead husband had liked.

At Marine Lines she almost lost one. It slipped, spun on the platform tiles, and the city held its breath, pretending not to watch. A schoolboy caught it mid-roll and handed it back without a word. She nodded; he nodded; nothing was said, and everything was.

By eleven-forty the bag was lighter and her shoulders heavier. The widow in building nine opened the door already crying, though nothing had happened yet — or perhaps everything had, years ago, and today was only the aftershock. Lata pressed the tiffin into her hands.

"Khaana garam hai, aunty. Aaj se main aaungi." The food is warm, aunty. From today, I will come.

The widow looked at her for a long second, as if recognising someone not quite present, and then she smiled the small, tired smile of women who have learned that the world arrives, eventually, by train.

Hero · Sec 2a

Descriptive Writing

Pick when the prompt is a place, an object or a photograph — when atmosphere matters more than plot.

Conventions
  • Anchors in all five senses, not just sight
  • Controlling mood held from opening to close
  • Movement through space — a camera, not a checklist
  • Specific nouns; adjectives used sparingly
  • Figurative language (simile, metaphor, personification) in every paragraph
  • No plot, but implied presence of a narrator
  • Ends on image, not explanation
The Fish Market at Four descriptive · 362 words

At four in the morning, Sassoon Dock is not a market yet — it is a rumour of one. The air tastes of salt and kerosene, and the sea, still half-asleep, rolls over in its bed of nets and mutters something only the gulls understand.

A single bulb above a weighing-scale throws a puddle of yellow light. Inside that puddle, crates of pomfret glint like coins spilled from some enormous, careless pocket. The ice beneath them cracks with a sound like tiny bones breaking politely. Beyond the bulb the dock is all shadow and silhouette — women squatting with their saris tucked between their knees, men in vests the colour of old tea, everyone busy in the dim, unhurried way of people who know the sun has never once been late.

Silver scales, sloshed seawater, the slap of slippers on slippery stone — the dock has its own language, hissed rather than spoken. A barefoot child carries a basket almost as tall as she is; she does not stagger. A crow argues with no one in particular. Somewhere a radio plays an old Kishore Kumar song so softly it could be a memory leaking out of the wood.

The boats begin to come in. They nose through the dark water one after another, tired mothers returning with the night's difficult children — prawns that twitch, crabs that click, eels that coil and accuse. The men on board heave them ashore in long, wet handfuls. Prices are called. Prices are dismissed. Fingers snap. A price is agreed.

And then, almost without anyone noticing, a thin pink ribbon uncurls along the horizon — pale, uncertain, the colour of the inside of a shell. The gulls rise first. The bulb is switched off. The market, which was a rumour, becomes a fact. By the time the city's alarms begin to ring, Sassoon Dock has already lived half a day, and is only just now pausing to stretch its silver, fish-scaled back.

Hero · Sec 2a

Monologue

Pick when you can hear a single voice in your head and the prompt hands you a crisis, a confession or a memory.

Conventions
  • One speaker, addressing self, audience or an unseen listener
  • Distinctive voice — rhythm, tic, vocabulary, register
  • Stage directions in italics or square brackets, used sparingly
  • Emotional arc: entry, shift, revelation
  • Breaks, pauses and self-corrections that mimic real thought
  • At least one moment where the speaker surprises themselves
  • Final line that lingers — a silence, an image, a return
Understudy monologue · 356 words

[A girl, seventeen, in a costume that doesn't quite fit. An empty auditorium. She is holding a half-eaten samosa.]

They said I'd go on if Ritika got sick. Ritika doesn't get sick. Ritika doesn't get tired. Ritika, I am fairly certain, does not even produce lactic acid like the rest of us mortals. I have watched her for three months from the wings, mouthing her lines into my collarbone, and I could perform this play standing on my head in a monsoon.

So today I did something. Don't look at me like that — I didn't poison her. Please. I put an extra spoon of sugar in her tea. [beat] Seven spoons. I told her the canteen aunty had made it specially, because she was the lead. I called her the lead. I smiled so hard my face hurt. She drank it. She smiled back. And then, at four o'clock, on cue, like a very obedient understudy herself, her stomach began to rewrite the schedule.

[She looks up, then down at the samosa.]

Here's the thing nobody tells you about getting what you want. It arrives bitterly sweet, like tea with seven spoons of sugar. It arrives wearing Ritika's face, and her mother crying in row four, and the whole auditorium looking past you because they came to see someone else. My name isn't even in the programme. They'll announce it before the curtain. "In today's performance, the role of Radha will be played by —" and then a pause while someone squints at a Post-it.

Is this ambition? Is this what the word feels like inside a seventeen-year-old chest? It feels less like fire and more like swallowing a small, grown-up version of myself, whole and still protesting.

[She puts the samosa down. Stands. Breathes.]

Places in five. I have waited three months. I have three hours to forgive myself. Or — better plan — three hours to be so good that I don't have to.

Hero · Sec 2a

Dialogue / Script

Pick when the prompt is a conflict between two people, or asks for a scene.

Conventions
  • Character names in caps or bold, left-aligned
  • Stage directions italicised and minimal
  • Distinct voices — no two characters should sound the same
  • Subtext: the real conversation under the spoken one
  • Conflict that escalates, not just continues
  • Beats of silence used as punctuation
  • Exits on a line, not an explanation
Report Card two-hander · 361 words

[A kitchen. Evening. AMMA is cutting onions. ARJUN, fifteen, slides a folded paper across the counter and sits very straight, as if straightness is a kind of defence.]

AMMA. Put it on the table, not in my hand. My hands are busy. (a beat) Busy like mine, beta, so that yours can be free later.

ARJUN. I got a five in math.

AMMA. (still cutting) Out of seven?

ARJUN. Out of seven.

AMMA. Out of seven is not bad.

ARJUN. Out of seven is not a seven.

[AMMA stops cutting. She does not turn around.]

AMMA. Your nana cut sugarcane. Your dada drove an auto. Your father fixes computers. You — you solve x. And you think five out of seven is not a seven?

ARJUN. Ritesh got seven.

AMMA. Ritesh is not my son.

ARJUN. Exactly.

[Silence. AMMA turns. The knife is still in her hand but pointed, now, at nothing.]

AMMA. Arjun, a five is not a wound, it's a door with a lock that's a little stuck. You push it again tomorrow.

ARJUN. And if it's stuck again tomorrow?

AMMA. Then the day after. (softer) You know what my mother told me on my wedding day? She said, "Beti, a grade is not a verdict, it is only a weather report."

ARJUN. (small smile) That is a terrible thing to say on a wedding day.

AMMA. It is a terrible thing to say on any day. (she picks up the report card) But you remember it, don't you?

ARJUN. I remember everything you tell me, Amma. That is actually my problem.

[AMMA laughs — a short, surprised laugh, the first of the evening. She slides the card back.]

AMMA. Go. Eat. Tomorrow the door.

ARJUN. Tomorrow the door.

[Lights down on onions, still waiting to be cut.]

Hero · Paper 1

Poetry (Free Verse)

Rarely a production option — but high-probability Paper 1 analysis. Learn the anatomy.

Conventions
  • Compressed image, not summary
  • Deliberate line breaks — pause, surprise, emphasis
  • Sound patterning (alliteration, assonance, consonance)
  • Controlling metaphor or image that returns
  • Turn (volta) somewhere in the middle or near the end
  • Title that does something — reframes the poem
  • Final line that rings, not concludes
Load-Shedding, 8:47 pm free verse · 44 lines / 358 words of commentary + poem

The fridge sighs first — it always does, the old socialist of the house, complaining loudest when the power leaves. Then the tubelight flickers, reconsiders, and the ceiling fan slows like a tired argument no one was winning anyway. Amma lights the candle she keeps in the third drawer beside the turmeric, and the kitchen becomes a small, bright darkness — the way grief is, the first evening. Outside, the lane remembers how to speak. A bicycle bell. A baby. A neighbour calling a neighbour by her full name for the first time since the wedding. The city forgets. The city forgets. The city forgets its electric manners and becomes, for eleven minutes, the village it used to be. I do my homework by the candle's throat-clear light. The equations lean, warm, almost affectionate. Algebra is easier by flame; the unknown is suddenly familiar, a cousin who visits in the dark. When the power returns — and it always returns, eventually, apologising in the language of humming — the tubelight bleaches the candle back into a piece of wax, a wick, a witness. Amma blows it out. A small grey comma of smoke punctuates the room, and the fridge resumes its old, reassured complaint. I keep doing my homework but the equations, ungrateful, have gone back to being equations.

Reading note. The poem's volta is "and it always returns, eventually" — the shift from outage as loss to outage as gift. The controlling metaphor (flame = intimacy, fluorescence = routine) is set up, inverted, and released. The anaphora "The city forgets" is the rhythmic heart.

Hero · Paper 1

Memoir / Autobiographical Extract

Frequent Paper 1 reading text — and a quiet favourite under "descriptive" in Sec 2a.

Conventions
  • First-person, retrospective voice — the older self remembering
  • A specific moment held in close focus, not a timeline
  • Double vision: child-perception and adult-understanding
  • Sensory detail anchored to real places and names
  • Honesty over flattery — the speaker is allowed to be wrong
  • A reflective sentence that earns its wisdom
  • Opens in scene, not in summary
The Summer of the Green Cycle memoir · 364 words

I was eleven the summer my father brought home the green cycle, and for a few weeks I believed, in the uncomplicated way only eleven-year-olds can, that the cycle was a form of love. It had a bell shaped like a teardrop and a saddle that smelled faintly of rubber and rain. It was, I now see, the shape of an apology we did not yet have words for.

Appa had missed most of that year. The phrase we used at home was "work in the Gulf", which in Kerala in those days was a kind of liturgy — a sentence you repeated so often the missing person became a blessing rather than an absence. When he came back for summer he brought a tin of powdered milk for my mother, a box of dates for my grandmother, and, for me, this green, astonishing machine.

I could not ride it. I had spent my entire life — all eleven years of it — failing to balance on anything that had fewer than four legs. My cousins mocked me with the gentle ferocity of cousins. My father, who had the patient hands of a man who had learned patience by being paid very little for it, held the saddle and walked beside me up and down the sandy lane behind our house.

"Keep looking at the end of the lane," he said. "Not at the front wheel. The cycle goes where your eyes go."

I think I have spent the rest of my life discovering how true that sentence was. The cycle goes where your eyes go, and so does almost everything else — fear, ambition, love. That afternoon I fell exactly seven times. On the eighth I did not fall. I looked up and the end of the lane was further than it had been, and my father's hand was no longer on the saddle. It had been gone, I later learned, for almost a full minute.

He watched me ride past the gate and out into the brown, sun-bitten afternoon, the coconut fronds clacking above me like applause that was somehow both for me and for him.

Hero · Sec 2b

Diary (Reflective)

Pick when the prompt asks for a personal response to an event — it is the safest Sec 2b option for Criterion D voice.

Conventions
  • Dated entry, often with place and time
  • First-person, present-tense sense of immediacy
  • Private register — free to be messy, honest, interrupted
  • Emotion on the page, not just around it
  • A turn or realisation mid-entry
  • Closing line that sounds like the diary being shut
  • Optional sign-off ("— A.")
Tuesday, 12:41 am reflective diary · 357 words

Tuesday. Past midnight, so technically Wednesday. The house is asleep and the fridge is doing that humming thing it does when it wants to be a pet.

I did not get the part. I am writing that first so I don't have to lead up to it like a bad speech. Ritika got it, which — obviously — Ritika got it. I clapped. I clapped loudly, the way you clap when you are trying to convince your own hands that you are fine.

Here is the strange thing, diary. I am sadder about the clapping than about the losing. The losing I can understand. The losing has a reason — Ritika was, annoyingly, excellent. But the clapping was a performance I gave for an audience of one (myself), and I did not even get a good review.

Ma asked if I wanted to talk about it and I said no in a voice that meant yes and she, being Ma, heard the yes. She brought me badam milk that was too hot and sat on the edge of the bed without saying anything. That is her whole technique. She waits for your feelings the way a fisherwoman waits for fish — patiently, as if the waiting itself were a kind of catch.

Eventually I cried a little. Not the cinematic kind. The quietly loud kind, where your shoulders do all the work and your face barely cooperates.

I keep thinking of what Appa said last month — that disappointment is just ambition finding out it has legs. I didn't understand it then. I think I almost understand it now. I wanted that part. The wanting did not go away because somebody else got it. The wanting is still here, walking around my ribs like a stubborn, well-lit tenant.

Is it childish to still want something after the door has closed? Or is it the whole point?

Anyway. The milk has skin on it now. I should sleep. Tomorrow I have to be nice to Ritika, and mean it.

— A.

The Peacock Who Learned to Listen fable · 352 words

In a forest where every tree had an opinion and the river had two, there lived a peacock called Pratham who was unusually beautiful and unusually certain of it. Each morning he walked the red path by the banyan and fanned his tail at the ferns, and the ferns, who were polite, bowed exactly as far as ferns are permitted to.

One afternoon an old tortoise named Bhooma crossed his path. She was small and dusty and, in Pratham's considered opinion, aesthetically disappointing.

"Move, grandmother," said Pratham, "you are in the way of my admirers."

Bhooma blinked once, slowly. "I do not see any admirers."

"They are there," said Pratham, waving his tail at the empty clearing. "They are simply too overwhelmed to clap."

Bhooma settled on the path as if she intended to stay for an era. "Peacock," she said, "tell me one thing you have heard this morning."

Pratham thought. He had heard his own wings. He had heard his own feet. He had heard, now that he considered it, an enormous and uninterrupted quantity of himself.

"I have heard," he said slowly, "many very important sounds."

"Name one."

He could not.

Bhooma nodded, unsurprised. "A tail is a thing you are given. A pair of ears is a thing you must earn." And she walked on, which for a tortoise took some time.

Pratham stood in the clearing until his shadow grew long. The ferns rustled without bowing; the river changed its second opinion; somewhere a koel argued with a crow about the correct way to end a sentence. He closed his tail — it made, for the first time in his life, a small, unnoticed sound — and listened.

He listened for such a long time that, by evening, he was the only creature in the forest who could tell you, accurately, what the forest sounded like. And the forest, quietly, began to admire him for it.

Moral: The loudest feather in the tail can be quieter than a listening ear.

How the Monsoon Learned to Knock myth · 356 words

Long before calendars, before clocks, before the first farmer drew a line around a field and called it his, the monsoon did not knock. It arrived, as weather once did, without any sense of appointment — sometimes at dawn, sometimes at midnight, sometimes not for three years together, and once, by mistake, twice in the same afternoon.

In those days the god of rains was a girl named Vaani. She rode on a cloud that obeyed her the way a good horse obeys a careful rider, and her laughter was the first thunder and the last. She was beloved, and she was, in the manner of those who are beloved too early, a little careless with other people's roofs.

One evening, restless, she let her cloud down upon a village that had no warning. The rice seedlings, newly planted, were drowned. The cattle scattered. An old weaver lost the sari she had been working on for her daughter's wedding, and her loom, and the last letter from her son. She lost a garment, and a trade, and a memory, all in one afternoon.

The weaver did not curse the god. That is not what weavers do. She walked to the highest hill in the district and spoke, as weavers speak, with the patience of someone who has untangled worse.

"Daughter," she said to the cloud, "I have no quarrel with your water. I have a quarrel with your manners. When you come to a house, knock first."

Vaani, who had never been told a truth so small, was ashamed. She sent back the cloud and, for three days, did not laugh at all. The sky kept its own counsel.

On the fourth day, far to the east, a breeze began — a dry, unremarkable breeze, the kind a child might feel on the back of the neck. Behind it came a low, polite rumble. Behind that, at last, the rain.

That rumble is what we still call, in the old language, the monsoon's knock. And a house that hears it before the water comes knows, even today, to open its doors.

The Girl Who Borrowed Light fairy tale · 358 words

Once, in a kingdom where the sun had grown tired and gone to bed early for many winters, there lived a girl called Neela who owned nothing except a small clay lamp and the habit of being kind to strangers. The lamp had been her grandmother's. It held one drop of oil — never more — and it burned, curiously, only when somebody else needed the light more than she did.

One cold evening three travellers came to her door.

The first was a merchant, heavy with coin, who complained that the road was too dark for counting. Neela lit the lamp, and the merchant counted his coins and went on without thanking her. The lamp did not refill.

The second was a minister, heavy with titles, who complained that the road was too dark for thinking. Neela lit the lamp, and the minister thought his important thoughts and went on without looking at her face. The lamp did not refill.

The third was a child, heavy with nothing, who was crying because he had lost his mother on the road and could not see his own hands in the dark. Neela lit the lamp. The child stopped crying. He held the lamp up to his face and said, "Your hands look tired."

Neela, who had not been noticed for a very long time, felt a small, astonished warmth open inside her chest, like a second, private lamp.

When the child had gone — his mother had been only half a field away — Neela looked down at her lamp. It was full. Not with oil. With a steady, kind, unhurried light that needed no wick at all.

The kingdom, it is said, grew warmer after that. The sun came back, eventually — the sun is shy, not unkind — but the old people say it was Neela's lamp that taught it the way.

And from that day to this, in that kingdom, a house that is kind to strangers is said to be "carrying Neela's light" — and nobody, not even the sun, ever arrives at such a house without knocking first.

The Cartographer of Borrowed Weather fantasy · 361 words

In the city of Saar-e-Aab, where it rained only on Thursdays and only on the rich, Ismet kept a shop that sold weather by the hour. He was the last cartographer of borrowed storms, and his map was not of land but of sky — a great parchment the size of a bedsheet, on which the clouds above the city moved, slowly and legally, from household to household.

The magic of Saar-e-Aab was simple and cruel. A family could buy, at the Council's office, the right to an hour of a particular weather — a cool drizzle for a wedding, a thick fog to hide a funeral, a spoonful of snow for a dying grandmother's last wish. The skies obeyed the coins, as skies have always, in every world, obeyed the coins of those who were paying attention.

Only one family in the old quarter — the Dervish sisters, weavers and orphans — had never bought any weather at all. For seventeen years their rooftop had held only whatever was left over: a warm wind nobody wanted, a pale sun on a Tuesday, the occasional confused drizzle that had lost its appointment.

It was the youngest Dervish, Halima, who came to Ismet's shop on a sunless morning in the month of Naar. She had no coins. She had a small wooden box and inside the box, carefully folded, a single thundercloud she had been saving since her mother's death.

"I want to give it back," she said.

Ismet, who had bought and sold a hundred thousand skies, had never in his life been offered one. He felt the map under his hand grow warm, the way a letter grows warm when it is finally addressed to the right person.

"You understand," he said carefully, "that if you give it back, the next storm that falls on the old quarter will be free. The Council will be furious."

Halima looked at him with the flat, certain gaze of orphans and cartographers. "Let them be furious," she said. "Weather was never theirs to sell." And outside, for the first time that Naar, thunder — unpaid for and unapologetic — began to roll across the rooftops.

The Last Person to Remember science fiction · 359 words

By the time the policy was announced, the Memory Bureau had already been operating for eleven years. Citizens between the ages of forty and seventy were offered a simple exchange: for a modest monthly credit against their tax, they would upload one personal memory a week to the civic archive. The memories were anonymised, they were assured. They were also — although the assurance was printed in smaller letters — removed.

Most people chose the credit. Nobody, the Bureau explained, missed a memory they no longer remembered having.

Amma was sixty-four when the Bureau officer came to the door. She made him tea, because she was the last person in our neighbourhood who still made tea for strangers, and she asked him, politely, to explain exactly what she would be giving up. He read from the script. He showed her the slider on his tablet. He offered her the starter credit of two thousand rupees.

"Which memories?" she said.

"You choose, ma'am. The system suggests low-utility ones — the ones you have not accessed in more than a year. Old phone numbers. Recipes you have written down. The names of childhood pets."

"And my husband's voice?"

The officer smiled. "That would be classified as high-utility. The system would never suggest it."

Amma nodded. She drank her tea. She told the officer she would think about it and then she told him she had thought about it and that her answer was no — not not yet, but no.

He was, by the training manual, obliged to ask why. She said, "A memory you do not use is not the same as a memory you do not need. A house has a cellar."

After he left she sat by the window for a long time. I came home from school and found her there. The tea was cold; the house was quiet; and in the low afternoon light she was whispering, very softly, a phone number I did not recognise — holding on to it, the way, I suppose, our whole family will one day hold on to her.

Suspense

Pick when the prompt is built on dread — what is just out of sight, just about to happen.

Conventions
  • Delayed revelation — withhold the monster

  • Short, clipped sentences at peak moments

  • Ordinary setting turned unfamiliar

The 11:42 to Nowhere suspense · 357 words

The last local from Churchgate was always, always, the 11:42. Everyone who worked the night shift knew it. The timetable knew it. The crows on the overhead wires, in their crow way, knew it.

So when Priya stepped onto the platform at Grant Road at 11:51, and the indicator still read 11:42 — ARRIVING, she stopped.

The platform was empty. This in itself was not strange. The small sounds of a station emptying — the scrape of a broom, the last shutter coming down at the bhel stall, the uncertain click of a fluorescent tube deciding whether to continue existing — all of that had already happened. What was strange was the absence of the absence. It was too quiet, and the quiet was listening back.

She checked her phone. 11:51. 11:51. 11:51.

The screen was not refreshing.

Down the track, from the direction of the city, a single headlight appeared. Priya had taken this train four hundred times. She knew — with the certainty of habit — what the approach of the 11:42 looked like. This was wrong. It was too bright. It was too silent. There was no rattle on the rails.

The light grew. It did not slow.

She stepped back from the yellow line.

And then — and this is the part she could not, afterwards, explain to the station-master, or to her mother, or to the young constable who took her statement with the patience of a man who had heard stranger things — the train, impossibly, passed through the station without stopping. Without a sound. Without, so far as she could see, any passengers.

Literary Devices

A device you can name is a device you can deploy. Every example below was written fresh — so that recognising them in an unseen text, and using them in your own, becomes a single habit.

Figurative & Comparative
Simile
Compares using like or as.
Her patience was like well water — deep, cold, and not always easy to reach.
Metaphor
Says one thing is another.
The exam hall was a courtroom in which I was both witness and accused.
Extended Metaphor
A metaphor sustained across multiple sentences or a whole passage.
The school was a ship. The principal stood at the helm, the corridors were decks, and every bell was a foghorn calling us to order.
Personification
Gives human qualities to the non-human.
The old fan in the corner coughed once and went back to its opinions.
Pathetic Fallacy
Nature reflecting a character's emotions.
As she read the results, the sky darkened, as though it too had been disappointed.
Hyperbole
Deliberate, obvious exaggeration.
I must have read that page six thousand times and it has never once read me back.
Litotes
Understatement by denying the opposite — not un-.
She was not unintelligent — she simply chose to apply her intelligence elsewhere.
Meiosis
Deliberate understatement to diminish something important.
"A slight disagreement," she called it; three shelves had been overturned.
Symbolism
An object that stands for an idea.
The green cycle, still leaning by the gate, had become the shape of his forgiveness.
Allegory
An entire story works as an extended symbol for a real-world idea.
The fable of the peacock is also a fable about politicians who speak but never listen.
Imagery (Visual)
Detail that paints for the eyes.
The kitchen glowed amber, the steel vessels catching the last light like a row of small moons.
Imagery (Auditory)
Detail that paints for the ears.
The temple bell rang once — a single bronze note that held the morning by the throat.
Imagery (Olfactory)
Detail that paints for the nose.
The corridor smelled of phenyl, chalk dust, and the particular sadness of reheated sambar.
Imagery (Gustatory)
Detail that paints for the tongue.
The coffee was bitter enough to be honest and sweet enough to drink.
Imagery (Tactile)
Detail that paints for touch.
The paper was warm, as though the printer had just breathed it out.
Imagery (Kinaesthetic)
Detail that evokes movement or bodily sensation.
Her stomach dropped — the lurch of a missed step on a staircase.
Oxymoron
Two contradictory words joined deliberately.
It was a loud silence, the kind only teenagers perfect.
Paradox
A seemingly contradictory statement that reveals a truth.
The more I studied, the less I understood — and the less I understood, the more I finally learned.
Pun
Play on a word with two meanings.
The mathematician's wife divorced him; she said he'd lost his common denominators.
Analogy
Extended comparison to explain an unfamiliar idea via a familiar one.
Revising without past papers is like practising basketball without a hoop — you move, but you don't score.
Conceit
An unusual, strikingly original metaphor (often literary).
Her memory was an old filing cabinet — the drawers stuck, the labels peeled, but everything was still inside, in order.
Metonymy
Replacing something with a related term — the crown for the king.
The bench ruled in her favour; the press disagreed.
Synecdoche
A part stands for the whole, or the whole for a part.
All hands were on deck. A dozen heads turned.
Sound Devices
Alliteration
Repeated consonant at the start of nearby words.
The bakery breathed butter, bread and belonging.
Assonance
Repeated vowel sound across nearby words.
The slow boat rolled into a known harbour.
Consonance
Repeated consonant sound within or ending words.
A pitter-patter matter of fact afternoon.
Onomatopoeia
Word imitates a sound.
The kettle whistled; the bread crackled; the day clicked on.
Sibilance
Hissing sound pattern — s, sh, z.
Silver scales slipped across the slick stone floor.
Plosive
Hard, explosive consonant sounds — b, d, g, p, t, k — for impact.
The bell barked, brief and brutal, and the boys bolted.
Fricative
Soft, hissing or buzzing consonants — f, v, th, z — for gentleness or unease.
The fire flickered, fading to a thin, fragile thread.
Cacophony
Harsh, discordant sounds creating tension.
The cracked clock clattered, clanged, and collapsed on the concrete.
Euphony
Smooth, pleasant sounds creating calm.
The willow leaned over the still water, trailing leaves like long, low lullabies.
Rhyme
Matching end-sounds for music and memorability.
A mind that reads is a mind that leads.
Internal Rhyme
Rhyme occurring within a single line.
He paced the space with a face that had aged a decade in a day.
Rhythm / Metre
A pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables.
da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM — iambic pentameter marches like a heartbeat.
Repetition
Same word or phrase for emphasis.
I waited. I waited. I waited until waiting became a kind of address.
Structural Devices
Anaphora
Repetition at the start of successive clauses.
We rose early. We rose tired. We rose as if rising were itself a kind of answer.
Epistrophe
Repetition at the end of successive clauses.
She would not speak of the war. Her mother would not speak of the war. The whole village would not speak of the war.
Symploce
Anaphora and epistrophe combined — same start and same end.
When there is talk of safety, there is no safety. When there is talk of freedom, there is no freedom.
Parallelism
Matching grammatical structure across phrases.
To read is to listen; to write is to reply; to revise is to apologise.
Antithesis
Contrasting ideas placed side by side.
We wanted silence, and were given only quiet.
Tricolon
Three-part list with rising impact.
She came, she saw, she filed a detailed complaint.
Climax (Gradatio)
A sequence that rises in importance or intensity.
He passed the test, he topped the class, he won the scholarship.
Anticlimax (Bathos)
A sudden, deflating drop after a build-up — for humour or irony.
He braved the storm, crossed the river, scaled the wall — and forgot his keys.
Chiasmus
Reversed parallel structure — AB → BA.
You forget what you want to remember, and you remember what you want to forget.
Asyndeton
Conjunctions removed for urgency.
Books, pens, nerves, hope — she packed them all.
Polysyndeton
Extra conjunctions added for rhythm or weight.
And the rain and the bell and the bus and the boy were all suddenly one thing.
Enjambment
A sentence runs across line breaks without punctuation (in poetry).
I walked across the field and / into a different year.
Caesura
A deliberate pause in the middle of a line (in poetry).
The door closed. || She did not look back.
Juxtaposition
Two contrasting images, ideas or scenes placed next to each other.
Outside, children played. Inside, a woman sat alone with a sealed envelope.
In Medias Res
Starting in the middle of the action, not the beginning.
The tiffin was already falling when she turned. Three seconds. Two hands. One chance.
Volta
A turn in argument or mood, especially in poetry.
And then — the power returned. And everything that mattered in the dark stopped mattering.
Circular Structure
The ending echoes or returns to the opening.
It began with a bicycle bell. It ended — years later, cities later — with the same bell, rusted.
Frame Narrative
A story within a story — an outer narrator introduces an inner tale.
My grandfather told me this. He said his grandfather told him. The story, it seems, is older than any of us.
Rhetorical & Persuasive
Rhetorical Question
A question posed for effect, not answer.
Is a home the building, or the argument you left in it?
Hypophora
A rhetorical question followed by the speaker's own answer.
Is there a solution? There is — and it begins with listening.
Ethos
Establishing the speaker's credibility or moral authority.
As someone who has worked in this community for twenty years, I can assure you…
Pathos
Appealing to the audience's emotions.
She was six years old. She walked two kilometres every day to drink clean water.
Logos
Appealing to logic, data and reason.
Sixty-two per cent of students surveyed said they would read more if the process were faster.
Imperative
A command-form verb that directs the reader.
Put down the phone. Pick up a pen. Begin.
Direct Address
Speaking to the reader or audience using "you".
You know this feeling. You have lived it. And you will live it again.
Inclusive Pronoun
Using "we" and "our" to unite speaker and audience.
We are the generation that was told to wait. Let us stop waiting.
Concession
Acknowledging the opposing view before refuting it.
Of course the policy has merits. But its merits are dwarfed by its costs.
Rebuttal
Directly countering an opposing argument.
Those who argue that children are too young to understand politics underestimate the children and overestimate the politics.
Aphorism
A short, memorable statement of truth.
A tail is a thing you are given. A pair of ears is a thing you must earn.
Epigram
A witty, paradoxical one-liner.
I can resist anything except temptation.
Narrative & Dramatic
Irony (Verbal)
Saying the opposite of what you mean.
The fire-safety poster caught fire during the drill, as if to illustrate itself.
Irony (Situational)
An outcome that is the opposite of what was expected.
The swimming instructor drowned on his day off.
Irony (Dramatic)
The audience knows something the character does not.
She opened the letter cheerfully; we already knew what it said.
Foreshadowing
A hint of what is to come.
The sky had the exact colour it had the evening Appa left; no one mentioned it.
Flashback
A jump to an earlier moment in time.
Before the interview, before the tie, before the train, there had been a green cycle.
Flash-forward
A jump to a future moment in time.
She would think of this morning for years — though she did not know it yet.
Dramatic Monologue
A single character speaking, revealing their inner self.
You think I wanted the part? I wanted what the part would prove.
Soliloquy
A character thinking aloud, alone on stage.
If I do this, I cannot undo it. If I don't — I'll never know.
Aside
A brief remark directed at the audience, unheard by other characters.
[to the audience] She believes me. That is the worst part.
Subtext
What is meant beneath what is said.
"It's fine." (It was not fine.)
Characterisation (Direct)
The narrator tells us what a character is like.
Amma was stubborn, generous, and incapable of admitting she was wrong.
Characterisation (Indirect)
We infer character from actions, speech, appearance.
She folded the report card into a paper plane and launched it down the corridor.
Motif
A recurring image, symbol or idea that develops a theme.
The green cycle appears three times — at the beginning, the middle, and the final paragraph.
Leitmotif
A phrase, image or idea repeated as a kind of signature.
"Tomorrow the door" — repeated by both mother and son, binding the scene.

Tone
The writer's attitude toward the subject — wry, solemn, tender.

Register
Level of formality — colloquial, neutral, formal.
"The initiative merits serious attention" (formal) vs "This idea deserves a proper look" (neutral).
Colloquialism
Informal, everyday language that adds warmth and voice.

Dialect / Vernacular
Regional speech used for authenticity.

Cutting irony intended to mock.

Polite softening of a hard truth.

Fixed phrase with non-literal meaning.

Allusion
An indirect reference to a well-known text, myth, event or person.
It was a Sisyphean task — she climbed the hill every morning, and every evening the stone rolled back down.

Satire
Using humour, irony or exaggeration to criticise and expose.

Advanced Grammatical Structures

Criterion D asks whether your sentences earn their complexity. Each card shows a simple sentence lifted into a structure an examiner rewards — plus the trap that costs the mark.

Complex Sentence (subordination)
Why it scores · syntactic range
The bell rang. Everyone left the classroom. As soon as the bell rang, everyone — even the ones who'd been dozing — left the classroom in a single, relieved wave.
Trap: dangling the subordinate clause from nothing ("Running fast, the bell rang" = the bell is running).
Compound-Complex
Why it scores · controlled range
I was tired. I finished the essay. Ma smiled. Although I was tired, I finished the essay; and when Ma finally smiled, I understood why.
Trap: comma splice. Use a semicolon or conjunction.
Second Conditional
Why it scores · hypothetical precision
If I have time, I will read more. If I had an hour of real quiet, I would read the way I used to — slowly, and on purpose.
Trap: mixing conditional types ("If I had time, I will read") reads like ESL-in-progress.
Third Conditional
Why it scores · regret / counterfactual
I didn't study. I failed. Had I studied the week before, rather than the morning of, I would have understood the paper instead of surviving it.
Trap: using "would have" in the if-clause. The if-clause takes "had + past participle".
Passive Voice (intentional)
Why it scores · register, objectivity
The committee made the decision on Monday. The decision was taken on Monday, and — in the way of such decisions — not discussed with those it most affected.
Trap: overusing passive in narrative writing. Reserve it for formal, reportorial, or deliberately evasive voices.
Reported Speech
Why it scores · tense backshift
"I will come tomorrow," he said. He said that he would come the following day, though something in his voice already suggested otherwise.
Trap: forgetting to backshift tense (will → would; tomorrow → the following day; this → that).
Relative Clause (defining & non-defining)
Why it scores · information density
I lost the book. It was my favourite. I lost the book that had been my favourite for years, a paperback whose spine had finally given up on me.
Trap: using commas with defining clauses ("The girl, who wore red, left" ≠ "The girl who wore red left"). Comma changes the meaning.
Participial Phrase
Why it scores · economy, movement
She closed the door. She walked away. Closing the door behind her without a sound, she walked away as if she had not been there at all.
Trap: a dangling participle — the participial subject must match the main clause subject.
Modal Stacking (subtle obligation)
Why it scores · register control
You must study more. You might want to consider whether you could, if you tried, begin to study a little earlier — not as a rule, but as a kindness to your future self.
Trap: stacking modals without reason bloats the sentence. Use for politeness, not padding.
Inversion (for emphasis)
Why it scores · literary register
I have never seen a sky like that. Never had I seen a sky like that — bruise-blue at the edges, apricot at the throat, absolute overhead.
Trap: inversion needs a negative adverbial trigger (never, rarely, not once, hardly had I…).
Subject–Verb Agreement (tricky)
Why it scores · accuracy under pressure
Each of the students are tired. Each of the students is tired; the committee itself is equally exhausted.
Trap: "each", "every", "neither", "one of" all take a singular verb, regardless of what comes between them.
Punctuation — Semicolon
Why it scores · sentence rhythm

Exam-Day Criterion D Checklist
  1. Read the task twice — before you read the text. Register first.
  2. Choose one controlling tone and do not drift from it.
  3. Plant at least four named devices — one must be structural (anaphora, tricolon, parallelism).
  4. Vary sentence length: short, short, long-and-breathing. Never three longs in a row.
  5. Use one conditional (second or third) and one passive, on purpose.
  6. Use one precise semicolon and one em dash — no more, no less.
  7. Upgrade at least five weak verbs on your re-read. "Said" is allowed; "very" is not.
  8. End on an image, a question, or a silence — never a summary.
  9. Check tense consistency across the whole piece before you leave the page.
  10. Word count: 340–380 is the sweet spot. Under 320 = no Criterion C. Over 400 = no time for a re-read.
